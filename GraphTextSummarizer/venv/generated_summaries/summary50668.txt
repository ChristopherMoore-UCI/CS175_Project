Arguments against the ratings system have, of course, been made many times before: by directors, producers, and film critics -- and, in 2006, by Kirby Dick in his documentary "This Film Is Not Yet Rated," which opened the door on the MPAA's antiquated maze of codes and restrictions. Yet "Bully" received an R rating, all because the F-word is used in it a handful of times. "Bully" is a portrait of good kids reduced to shells of themselves by a climate of toxic hate and fear. That the MPAA is content to keep a film as humane and as deeply unsensational as "Bully" away from most of the adolescents whose lives it could possibly help is a folly and outrage.
