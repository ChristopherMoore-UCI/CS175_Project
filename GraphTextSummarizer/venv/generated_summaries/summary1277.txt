As part of a deal with the IMF, the government cut subsidies on basic goods and riots erupted in southern Jordan, quickly turning into more general protests against the government. But even then, there was plenty of grumbling in Jordan -- over gas prices, unemployment, pervasive corruption. The tribes don't want to see the largely urban Muslim Brotherhood -- which derives much of its support from Jordan's Palestinian population -- gain power at their expense. But the threat of more unrest beckons, and could deter what little foreign investment Jordan attracts. Osama Al Sharif -- an Amman-based commentator and syndicated columnist -- says the  economic crunch is merging with evermore strident demands for political change, now led by the Muslim Brotherhood.
