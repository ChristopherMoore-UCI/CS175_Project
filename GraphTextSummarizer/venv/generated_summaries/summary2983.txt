Polling had shown American support for the two Panama Canal treaties, but by 1978, the American people had switched and agreed with Reagan and the conservatives -- and not Carter. Opinion: Hilary Clinton, RNC and freedom of the press Into this void stepped former California Gov. Ronald Reagan, who launched a national campaign to oppose the giveaway. Control of the canal was vital to American military interests, especially with Soviet control of Cuba and designs on Central America. For several years, the U.S. government had been making plans to yield control and ownership of the Panama Canal to Omar Torrijos, the dictator of the Panama. Years later, Carter himself acknowledged the potency of the canal issue in the election of 1980, which not only saw the rise of Reagan, but the loss of scores more Democrats who had voted for the treaties.
