Around 80% of demolished machiya in the city are replaced by modern houses and high-rise buildings, according to the University of Kyoto, while the majority of those that remain have suffered some loss to their original facades. Moussas first had to learn from carpenters and craftsmen the special skills needed to build machiya before he could draw up practical renovation plans. "This one housing type has been developing over 1,200 years, continuously adapting to the different lifestyles, adapting and changing," says American designer Geoffrey Moussas who over a decade has renovated over 30 machiya. We view our lives like software, something forever changing then this traditional architecture is the hardware, the very structure that gives Japanese life its foundation. That enduring love of old homes is not universal in Japan.
