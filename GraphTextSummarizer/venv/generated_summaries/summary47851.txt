The Marathon is usually a 26.2-mile party -- "the old, drunk uncle of Boston sports, the last of the true festival events," Massachusetts native and Boston-area resident Charles P. Pierce wrote on Grantland. A line of Boston police officers extended out across Boylston Street. Union laborers stood beside police and other Boston residents, their heads bowed, as the city sat eerily quiet until a harp's song sliced the silence. But, as the Twitter hashtag had it, #BostonStrong. "Boston is a tough & resilient town," said one sign near Boylston Street. "It's going to be a lot different next year," said Karen Russell, a Cambridge resident who works in Boston.
