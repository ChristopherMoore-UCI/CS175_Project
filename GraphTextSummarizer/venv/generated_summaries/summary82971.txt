Kaguri says that by giving grandmothers access to microfunds, allowing them to start small businesses and make money, more children have access to an education. He'd often come back to Uganda, passing out school supplies to children. Kaguri says he felt fortunate to have the financial means to help his brother's children financially, but in many similar cases, children end up homeless. "(We) take care of nearly 600 children in school," he said. Of the students at his schools, Kaguri estimates that 65% of them are being raised by their grandmothers, many of whom are often without adequate health care, finances or basic housing.
