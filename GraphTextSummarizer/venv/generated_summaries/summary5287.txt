In the 1970s, however, credit rating agencies started charging the issuers of new investments fees for ratings. The decisions of credit rating agencies also have a ripple effect through the market: In the wake of the Greece downgrade, investors across the globe started rethinking investment in other governments' bonds and began selling off more risky investments. Critics complain the agencies have lost their ability to independently judge the risk on certain investments -- especially in light of AAA ratings given to mortgage-backed securities that imploded when defaults on U.S. home loans shot up, triggering the financial crisis. Investors across the world look to credit ratings agencies to judge where to place their bets in the market. Credit ratings agencies, too, have revamped their procedures and have argued their ratings are merely opinions -- it's up to the markets to decide.
