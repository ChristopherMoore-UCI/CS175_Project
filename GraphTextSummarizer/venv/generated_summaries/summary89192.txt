"'Wa' means Japanese, 'shoku' means 'to eat' or 'meal' or anything food-related," explains cooking instructor and "washoku" enthusiast Reiko Yoshikawa. Earlier this month, UNESCO, the U.N. cultural organization, added traditional Japanese cuisine, or "washoku," into its Intangible Cultural Heritage list. Japanese cultural asset to be listed on UNESCO's heritage list, which also includes the country's theater forms such as Kabuki, Noh and Bunraku. Melons are highly prized, and those grown in Japanese greenhouses are considered the perfect fruit and can command high prices. "First of all, it means happiness, the gathering, of being together," Yoshikawa says.
